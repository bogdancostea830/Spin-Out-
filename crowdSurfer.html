Spin Out!

The Crowd Surfer

Between the aviators and the bangs down to your chin, no one can tell where you’re looking. There are more patches on your denim vest than there are stars in the sky. You love to get active and, frankly, violent at concerts. Distorted guitars and badass riffs are mandatory for any piece of music that should attempt to cross your ears.

Bad Brains - Bad Brains (1981)

Argued by some as being the greatest punk album of all time. Bad Brains were pioneers of hardcore punk, and their fast and rough DIY sound is iconic. The album cover illustration, called the Capitol Strike, is a famous symbol in the punk community on stickers, t-shirts, and patches. It also demonstrates the ties between the punk and reggae movements through the inclusion of tracks like “Jah Caling.”

The Jins - The Jins (2017)

The Jins are a Vancouver pop-punk band whose frontman looks and sounds remarkably like Kurt Cobain, which begs some questions of reincarnation and the supernatural that we won’t address here. The point is, their music is awesome, with a semi-grunge vibe applied to the energy of a pretty good mood. I have reason to suspect they may be called The Jins as short for the pigeons. Just a hunch.

System of a Down - Toxicity (2001)

Any attempt to put System into an existing genre falls short somewhere, because no one does it quite like they do. Their lyrics are thoughtfully written and intelligent in many ways, sung atop complete arrangements that incorporate elements of traditional Armenian sounds into hard, fast, American metal. Toxicity in particular is perhaps their most successful album, for singles like “Chop Suey!,” “Aerials,” and, of course, the title track.

Rage Against the Machine - Rage Against the Machine (1992)

Rage Against the Machine is known for their incorporation of funk and blues elements into their energetic, anti-establishment rap metal, and their self-titled is far and away their best manifestation of this sound, earning it #221 on the Rolling Stone list. “Killing In the Name,” “Bombtrack,” “Bullet In the Head,” and “Township Rebellion” (and all of the rest) are full of raw, angry energy and crazy Tom Morello guitar parts.

Prophets of Rage - Prophets of Rage (2017)

Prophets of Rage are a supergroup formed of the former members of Rage Against the Machine and Audioslave that leans heavier into the rap half of rap metal. They combine Rage Against the Machine’s signature instrumental style with energetic rap of a kind that is reminiscent but still distinct from Zack de la Rocha’s style.

the smalls - the smalls (1993)

This is for those disappointed with the exclusion of a Metallica album (I’ve only listened to 72 Seasons all the way through and I can’t in good conscience recommend it). the smalls are an Albertan hardcore punk band whose sound swings between Bad Brains and early Metallica (Kill ‘em All era) with speed, energy, and thoughtful composition. Also worth checking out their bassist, Corb Lund’s solo stuff (it’s country but it's good country).

Nirvana - In Utero (1993)

Sitting at #173 on the Rolling Stone list, In Utero is arguably Nirvana’s best album (even though Nevermind technically ranks higher.) It features the same raw, heavy, grungy sound from their debut album Bleach, but with stronger composition and writing, and with Dave Grohl on drums. On this tracklist, you will not find a single skip.

Dinosaur Jr. - You’re Living All Over Me (1987)

Dinosaur Jr. were pioneers for today’s indie rock sound. Their album You’re Living All Over Me employs a raw, heavy grunge energy that feels fresh and different even today. If you’re a fan of Nirvana, Alice In Chains, Queens of the Stone Age, and the like, you should definitely give this one a spin.

The Offspring - Americana (1998)

At the intersection of frosted-tip pop punk and frosted-tip hardcore punk lies The Offspring. Americana is the source of some of their most famous tracks, like “Pretty Fly (For A White Guy)”, “The Kids Aren’t Alright,” and “Why Don’t You Get A Job”, and also some extremely hard B-sides like “She’s Got Issues” and “Walla Walla.”

Korn - Follow the Leader (1998)

Even if you know nothing about Korn or nu metal in general, odds are you have heard “Freak On a Leash.” Undoubtedly Korn’s best known album, with a feature from Fred Durst (of Limp Bizkit fame, for the uninitiated), if you like metal or hip hop or both, you owe it to yourself to spin this if you haven’t before.

Silverstein - Discovering The Waterfront (2005)

One of the big names in Canadian hardcore emo. Silverstein’s polished, but still heavy sound goes swimmingly together with Shane Told’s almost stereotypically emo vocals and impressive false chord. Their riffs and lyrics are a sound to behold, best exemplified in tracks like “My Heroine,” “Your Sword Versus My Dagger,” and of course the title track.

Pearl Jam - Ten (1991)

Pearl Jam stand out from their contemporaries for a different approach to their songwriting, favoring a crushing sincerity over the vulgar sarcasm of bands like Nirvana and Soundgarden’s lyrics. Placing #160 on the Rolling Stone’s top 500 albums, Ten became a 90s grunge classic for its hits, like “Even Flow” (which is a masterclass in harmonizing two guitars), “Alive,” “Black,” and “Jeremy,” but its B-sides are arguably as good.

Soundgarden - Louder than Love (1989)

Louder than Love was Soundgarden’s sophomore album, and is criminally underrated today. It has a rougher sound compared to Superunknown, Badmotorfinger, and anything Chris Cornell ever did with Audioslave, and it shows, interestingly enough, some of grunge’s roots in glam rock, having been contemporary with Mother Love Bone’s only album Apple.

Mannequin Pussy - Perfect (2021)

Mannequin Pussy is a neo-grunge band from Philadelphia with a diverse sound that can range from fast, heavy, and screamy, like the title track of this EP; to slower, more emotional tracks like “Darling.” Certainly one of the cooler bands active today, and this EP (and really their whole discography) is certainly worth a spin.

Linkin Park - Hybrid Theory (2000)

Hybrid Theory is a classic of 90’s-2000’s nu metal that needs no introduction (but I’ll introduce it anyway). Even besides the hits that are “Papercut,” “Crawling,” and “In The End,” the album is full of track after track of heavy, thoughtfully crafted nu metal of the highest quality.

AC/DC - Back in Black (1980)

Few bands are as iconic as AC/DC. At #84 on the Rolling Stone top 500, Back in Black features some of the best examples of AC/DC’s iconic hard rock sound. “Hells Bells” is one of the strongest opening tracks you’ll hear, and thereafter you will not skip a single track for the remainder of its 41 minute runtime. And of course the title track needs no introduction.

Nirvana - Nevermind (1991)

Everyone’s favorite Nirvana album. It isn’t #6 on the Rolling Stone list for no reason, and a few of those reasons are the number of hits on it. It stands out against the rest of Nirvana’s discography as one of their most diverse albums. Some might call it too commercial but that’s what got them famous with “Smells Like Teen Spirit,” “Lithium,” and “Something In The Way.”

The Warning - Keep Me Fed (2024)

A Peruvian hard rock band made up of three sisters, all of whom are very talented musicians. There is not a single skip on this record, but the best of the tracks are “Six Feed Deep,” “S!CK,” and “Hell You Call A Dream.” They’ve been growing in popularity since touring with Foo Fighters and the internet’s particular interest in Alejandra, their bassist.

Nine Inch Nails - Pretty Hate Machine (1989)

To a non-fan, NIN just sounds like an angrier Depeche Mode (and that’s awesome already), but those who know them will know that they are responsible for some of the heaviest, crunchiest, nastiest industrial rock you can get outside of Germany. It is well deserving of its place on the Rolling Stone top 500 (at #453).

Nine Inch Nails - The Downward Spiral (1994)

The cooler older brother of Pretty Hate Machine (even though it's not as old). The Downward Spiral’s tracklist houses both “Closer,” which is undeniably one of the horniest songs of the 90s, and “Hurt,” the original version of the song famously, hauntingly covered by Johnny Cash. The album sits at #122 on the Rolling Stone list with a stanky, angry, sexy vibe like you will not find anywhere else.
