Spin Out!

The Crowd Surfer

Between the aviators and the bangs down to your chin, no one can tell where you’re looking. There are more patches on your denim vest than there are stars in the sky. You love to get active and, frankly, violent at concerts. Distorted guitars and badass riffs are mandatory for any piece of music that should attempt to cross your ears.

Primus - Pork Soda (1993)

Primus is famous (or perhaps notorious) for Les Claypool’s complex bass parts, which incorporate a lot of tapping, slapping, and dissonance to achieve their signature sound. It’s interesting to think about how Les Claypool grieves through his lyrics when listening to tracks like “Bob” or “Jerry Was A Race Car Driver” off their album Sailing The Seas Of Cheese, contrasting dark and traumatic events with weird, strange instrumentation.

TOOL - Lateralus (2001)

If Rush is the first step in one’s prog education, TOOL comes soon after. Beyond the theoretical complexity of the arrangements, the members of TOOL are also known for a preposterous level of skill at their respective instruments, seen clearly in tracks like “Schism,” where Justin Chancellor’s hand moves across the bass like a motor.

Rush - Moving Pictures (1981)

This is one of those albums you have to have listened to at least once. Within it are some of Rush’s best tracks of all time, such as the exemplary jam track that is “YYZ,” or the iconic and widely known “Tom Sawyer” and “Limelight.” If you consider yourself a prog enjoyer in any capacity, this is required reading at #379 on the Rolling Stone list.

Brass Camel - Camel (2025)

A phenomenal prog rock band from Vancouver. Brass Camel has a sound reminiscent of, but distinct from, Rush, also incorporating light elements of funk to distinguish themselves sonically. Their 11 minute track “Zealot” off Camel is one of my favorite prog tracks of all time. If you like the album, you should also check out their cover of Sabrina Carpenter’s “Please Please Please”!

Volbeat - God Of Angels Trust (2025)

Volbeat’s latest album. They are a Danish metal band whose sound is wildly diverse, but still instantly recognizable, owing to Michael Poulsen’s unmistakable voice and Jon Larsen’s impossibly complex drum parts, which seem to change completely every fourth bar. Their 2010 album Beyond Hell/Above Heaven is also worth listening to.

Truckfighters - Gravity X (2005)

Truckfighters have an immediately recognizable sound characterized by their groovy, running drum rhythms and layered guitars that create a sense of speed and motion without necessarily being very fast tempo. The sounds and tones they use are perfectly suited for the vibes they create through their album covers and track titles, to the point where the music seems to create its own world and invite you in.

Pink Floyd - The Wall (1979)

It’s Pink Floyd. It’s implicitly awesome. With an impressive 80 minute runtime across two discs, it represents some of Pink Floyd’s best and most iconic work, including the “Another Brick in the Wall” trilogy, “Hey You,” “Comfortably Numb,” and everything in between. At #129 on the Rolling Stone list, The Wall is one of those albums you have to listen to at least once in your life.

The Mars Volta - Deloused in the Comatorium (2003)

A slightly deeper cut, but an amazing work of music. Listening to the entire album front to back is one of the nicest things you can do for yourself if you know anything about music. The Mars Volta combines innovative instrumentation that bends the rules of music theory to their limit with impressive vocals singing lyrics that only Cedric Bixler-Zavala could write.

The Who - Tommy (1969)

The Who’s most iconic album, it tells the story of an eponymous protagonist who, despite his traumatically induced deafness, blindness, and muteness, becomes a pinball superstar. At #190 on the Rolling Stone list, this bizarre but captivating rock opera has earned all of its acclaim and adaptations for the screen and stage.

Greta Van Fleet - From the Fires (2017)

This one is for the Zeppelin fans who were disappointed with their exclusion from this board (I just haven’t listened to an entire album from them yet.) If you’re of the opinion that there hasn’t been any good music since the 70’s, you should definitely check out Greta Van Fleet. Their sound takes a lot of inspiration from Zeppelin’s style, but in a way that is unique and refreshing.

Angine de Poitrine - Vol.II (2026)

Klek plays live drums perfectly in time while Khn layers sometimes a dozen loops on his double-necked, microtonal guitar-bass and twists the knobs of his guitar pedals with his toes. To say nothing of the motor skills, the amount of skill and practice that goes into playing instruments at this level is insane, and produces music unlike anything you could hear anywhere else.

The Fearless Flyers - The Fearless Flyers V (2025)

The Fearless Flyers are a jam band made up of Cory Wong, Joe Dart, Mark Letteri, and Nate Smith. These four prolific musicians come together every once in a while to release a mind-blowing funk record, the most recent of which (V) stands out for using sounds and sensibilities not uncommon in Rush-inspired prog rock.

Masayoshi Takanaka - SEYCHELLES (1976)

You’re going to want a pair of skinny sunglasses and a mimosa for this one. Takanaka is famous for his beachy city pop (and his surfboard guitar), of which this album is a prime specimen. Takanaka is one of those musicians whose music it’s impossible to be in a bad mood with.

Snarky Puppy - We Like It Here (2014)

Snarky Puppy is a band with a lot of members, known for an incredibly diverse sonic palette and impeccable grooves. They incorporate cultural sounds from all over the world into a jazz fusion sound unlike any other. The difference in style they’re able to achieve between “Shokufan” and “Tio Macaco” with neither sounding out of place on the album speaks to their ability and vision.

Tinariwen - Elwan (2017)

Tinariwen are a band of Tuareg musicians indigenous to the Sahara known for their melding of their ancestral styles of music with western rock and blues sounds to create what they call desert blues. The result is something unlike anything you’ve ever heard. The atmosphere the music creates is intoxicating, as if the heat of the desert sun radiates from your speakers. Absolutely a must-hear.

Phoenix - Mugur de Fluier (1974)

Phoenix was a Romanian prog band that famously spoke out against communism through their lyrics, using metaphors derived from Romanian folklore and Dacian mythology. Their music incorporated a lot of sounds, instruments, and rhythms from Romanian folk music into odd time signatures and complex riffs. The traditional Romanian flute played in the album’s title track hits just as hard every time.

Jacob Collier - Djesse Vol. 4 (2024)

Jacob Collier is famous for his intimate understanding of (and feel for) the deepest and most complex facets of music theory, and he applies them in the Djesse series to create a frankly overwhelming body of transcendental music. Definitely worth a spin, but be mentally prepared for the sheer maximalism. There’s also a cover of “Bridge Over Troubled Water” on there!

WILLOW - empathogen (2024)

empathogen lands firmly in the pop camp, but the use of 7/4 time and compositions that fill space in just the right way give it a very progressive feel. The jazz elements add yet another dimension, and the whole album stands out as a work created with so much love for the art and knowledge of the theory. It is liable to take you places.

American Football - American Football (LP1) (1999)

American Football is a midwest emo band that incorporates many math rock elements, such as odd time signatures and complicated instrumentation. “Never Meant” is maybe one of the greatest songs ever, and its main riff in 6/4 and unusual tuning hits every time. They disbanded shortly after LP1’s release, during which time it became insanely popular in midwest emo communities, which thankfully led to their eventual return with LP2 in 2016.

Covet - technicolor (2020)

Covet is one of the strongest math rock bands currently active, and technicolor while not their most popular release, I believe to be their best. Yvette Young’s riffs have the perfect mix of twinkly complexity and feel. She is indubitably one of the greatest guitarists of our time, and her band supports her perfectly to create an auditory experience like no other.
