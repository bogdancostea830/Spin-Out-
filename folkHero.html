Spin Out!

The Folk Hero

You have an old guitar, a cheap harmonica, and something to say; and your playlist shows it. You love Bob Dylan, Simon & Garfunkel, and you’ll even dabble in country when it’s Dolly Parton singing. You value the message over the music, preferring minimalist and acoustic arrangements with quality lyrics to overcomplicated compositions.

Bob Dylan - Blood on the Tracks (1975)

Blood on the Tracks stands out slightly when compared to some of Dylan’s better known songs as having a slightly (but noticeably) darker and more mature sound. At an impressive #9 on the Rolling Stone list, it is well appreciated, and reasonably so, as evidence of Dylan’s maturation as an artist.

Simon & Garfunkel - Bridge Over Troubled Water (1970)

One of the most iconic works from THE most iconic folk duo of all time, Bridge Over Troubled Water sits at #172 on the Rolling Stone list. It earns this position through banger after banger, including a title track so awesome that Elvis Presley covered it. Fun fact for those who didn’t know, Presley was one of the artists that inspired Simon & Garfunkel since their high school days. That’s pretty neat.

Jake Minch - George (2025)

As Minch’s first LP, George does not disappoint. When I first heard it, I put it on on the bus ride home and I nearly started crying. Jake Minch has this way of crafting his songs in such an intimate and personal way that it really puts you into exactly how he probably felt writing them, which is usually equally parts nostalgic, hopeful, and unbearably depressed. An underappreciated gem of indie folk.

Phoebe Bridgers - Stranger in the Alps (2017)

Perhaps controversial to include this over Bridgers’ other album, Punisher but Stranger in the Alps is the more memorable of the two. Such titanic bangers as “Smoke Signals,” “Motion Sickness,” “Scott Street,” and “You Missed My Heart” are liable to make you cry like your first heartbreak. Bridgers’ hauntingly beautiful voice and indie instrumentals make her one of the best in the genre today. And she’s also part of Boygenius.

Elio Mei - To Exist With You (2023)

An unfortunately well-hidden gem of indie folk. Elio Mei offers a tooth-achingly sweet, heart-warmingly intimate record of soft, gentle, earnest songwriting like you’ve never heard before. With a lighthearted silliness reminiscent of Kimya Dawson’s work, packaged in gorgeous vocals and instrumentals, and without a single skip, To Exist With You gets nowhere near the attention it deserves.

The Softies - Holliday In Rhode Island (1994)

The Softies are one of the most aptly-named bands I know, in that the one word I would use to describe their sound is ‘soft.’ Through some of the prettiest vocals in the genre, together with twinkly, clean-toned guitars, they create music that calms, soothes, and uplifts like no other. I’ve fallen asleep to their music on more than a few occasions.

Lucy Dacus - Forever Is A Feeling (2025)

One third of the band Boygenius and an amazing artist in her own right, Lucy Dacus consistently puts out some of the saddest, most heartbreaking indie music of today. Forever Is A Feeling is my personal favorite album of hers, full of exemplary craftsmanship, devastating lyricism, and a Hozier feature you didn’t know you needed. If you’re a fan of music that puts you in your feelings, definitely give this one a spin.

James - Laid (1993)

An iconic folk record of the 90’s, with a humorous and well-crafted title track that is hard not to enjoy. In fact, the whole album is well-crafted, with tracks like “Dream Thrum,” “One Of The Three,” “Knuckle Too Far,” and et cetera. The record also features live recordings of a couple of the songs, which provide new takes on what by that point in the album will be new favorites of the genre.

Nirvana - MTV Unplugged in New York (1994)

Its position at #279 on the Rolling Stone list is especially impressive considering MTV Unplugged is a live album, where a vast majority (if not all, I didn’t count) of the rest of the list is made up of studio albums. The album features some of Nirvana’s best performances of their own songs, as well as several Meat Puppets covers and a Bowie song. Top shelf stuff!

Blackbird Raum - Swidden (2010)

Folk as a cultural movement is known, in part, for its political messaging. Another such cultural movement is punk, and Swidden sits right in that happy middle between the two. With raspy, yelling vocals over accordions, acoustic guitars, and a juicy juicy snare drum, Blackbird Raum craft a sound you can’t really get anywhere else. Bangers like “Witches,” “Shot Coplifting,” and, really, everything else on the album, make it criminally underrated.

Joyce Manor - I Used To Go To This Bar (2026)

Despite their shortcomings (not being able to write a song longer than two minutes, 30), Joyce Manor manages to be an iconic member of multiple genres, namely Midwest emo, pop-punk, and, with their newest release, indie folk. The sound they achieve here is really thoughtful, polished, and unlike anything they’ve ever made before. Definitely worth checking out.

Blues Traveler - Four (1994)

The purest concentrate of feeling good. Blues Traveler is music for good weather and good vibes, combining John Popper’s audibly smiling vocals and joyous harmonica with one of the tightest bands in the observable universe. If you’ve ever experienced a gorgeous, sunny day and not known what could possibly make it any better, try spinning this holy text of roots rock and find out.

Tom Petty - Full Moon Fever (1989)

Petty’s best known record and #298 on Rolling Stone’s top 500 albums of all time. A robust tracklist is made up of a majority of Petty’s best songs, with such bangers as “Free Fallin’,” “I Won’t Back Down,” and “Runnin’ Down A Dream”, and of course the B-Sides are also amazing.

Fleetwood Mac - Rumours (1977)

Well deserving of its #7 spot on the Rolling Stone list, there is not one of Rumours’ 11 tracks that isn’t amazing. The stylistic range of the record from “Dreams” to “Don’t Stop” to “The Chain” is exemplary, all the while it still manages to feel uniform and complete.

Phoenix - Mugur de Fluier (1974)

Phoenix was a Romanian prog band that famously spoke out against communism through their lyrics, using metaphors derived from Romanian folklore and Dacian mythology. Their music incorporated a lot of sounds, instruments, and rhythms from Romanian folk music into odd time signatures and complex riffs. The traditional Romanian flute played in the album’s title track hits just as hard every time.

Tinariwen - Elwan (2017)

Tinariwen are a band of Tuareg musicians indigenous to the Sahara known for their melding of their ancestral styles of music with western rock and blues sounds to create what they call desert blues. The result is something unlike anything you’ve ever heard. The atmosphere the music creates is intoxicating, as if the heat of the desert sun radiates from your speakers. Absolutely a must-hear.

Lord Huron - Strange Trails (2015)

This one’s for the Fleetwood Mac fans. Lord Huron’s particular brand of indie folk draws clear inspiration from the classic folk rock band, with occasional twists of outlaw country and modern touches that are incredibly refreshing and very pleasing to the ear.

Corb Lund - Cabin Fever (2012)

An Albertan musician, former bassist of the smalls, Corb Lund has an intimate and deeply respectful understanding of music. He puts all thought and care into every song he’s ever touched with his guitar, and you get to see great examples of this in Cabin Fever, where his outstanding musicianship shines through even in the humorous tone of tracks like “Bible on the Dash” and “The Gothest Girl I Can.”

Colter Wall - Imaginary Appalachia (2015)

Imaginary Appalachia is my primary defense against people who truly believe there is no good country music. Wall stands out against his peers in the genre by forsaking the superficial lyrics and overproduction in favor of something that feels like what modern country was always trying to be: honest, modest, and working-class. His newest album is pretty good too!

Marty Robbins - Gunfighter Ballads And Trail Songs (1959)

In this, one of the most important and iconic country records of all time, Marty Robbins provides his interpretations of several traditional folk classics in his gorgeous, steady, unmistakable voice. His renditions of “Cool Water” and “Big Iron” alone make the album worth spinning. An important reminder for those who would doubt it that there is good country music in the world.
