Spin Out!

Summer Salt - Happy Camper (2018)

Self-described as “trop-pop”, Summer Salt employs SoCal feelgoodery with clean, classic-sounding guitars for a sound that goes very well with a glass of lemonade and a chaise longue on a sunny summer day. Their subsequent albums drift into a darker vibe, which is certainly also worth listening to, but Happy Camper’s easy listening is what makes it my personal favorite.

Janis Joplin - Pearl (1971)

A preposterously well-crafted and curated record combining Joplin’s irreplicable voice with masterfully performed blues and folk arrangements. The album’s craft and iconic tracks such as “Cry Baby,” “Me and Bobby McGee,” and “Mercedes Benz” are what earn it #259 on the Rolling Stone top 500.

Hot Freaks - Hot Freaks (2013)

Modern indie doo-wop. Hot Freaks have an upbeat, feelgood sound that is sure to resonate with any fans of the earlier Beatles, Buddy Holly, The Kinks, and other such poppier rock artists. They combine a 50’s rockabilly attitude with 21st century indie rock grooves and sounds to produce music that will cure any acute negative feeling.

AC/DC - Back in Black (1980)

Few bands are as iconic as AC/DC. At #84 on the Rolling Stone top 500, Back in Black features some of the best examples of AC/DC’s iconic hard rock sound. “Hells Bells” is one of the strongest opening tracks you’ll hear, and thereafter you will not skip a single track for the remainder of its 41 minute runtime. And of course the title track needs no introduction.

Peach Pit - Magpie (2024)

Peach Pit is one of the more successful indie bands to have come from Vancouver in recent times, and this is owed to their polished, extremely well crafted sound. Magpie especially shows clear influence from The Beatles’ earlier sound, but still with a flair that is unique to them. The riff at the start of “St. Mark’s Funny Feeling” is straight out of Abbey Road.

The Doors - The Doors (1967)

The Doors’ self titled finds itself comfortably in the top 100, at #86 on the Rolling Stone list. With “Break on Through,” “Soul Kitchen,” and one of the biggest hits at the height of their career, “Light My Fire,” their groovy, psychedelic, bass-guitarless sound is iconic, timeless, and especially great for road trips.

Tom Petty - Full Moon Fever (1989)

Petty’s best known record and #298 on Rolling Stone’s top 500 albums of all time. A robust tracklist is made up of a majority of Petty’s best songs, with such bangers as “Free Fallin’,” “I Won’t Back Down,” and “Runnin’ Down A Dream”, and of course the B-Sides are also amazing.

Greta Van Fleet - From the Fires (2017)

This one is for the Zeppelin fans who were disappointed with their exclusion from this board (I just haven’t listened to an entire album from them yet.) If you’re of the opinion that there hasn’t been any good music since the 70’s, you should definitely check out Greta Van Fleet. Their sound takes a lot of inspiration from Zeppelin’s style, but in a way that is unique and refreshing.

Spencer Sutherland - The Drama (2024)

Spencer Sutherland captures an incredibly unique style and presence in his music that is equal parts hard rock, classic rock, and Freddie Mercury, with just enough modern flair. Glamorous, campy, and generally awesome, The Drama is definitely worth a listen if you want something classic, but have listened to all of the classics.

Lord Huron - Strange Trails (2015)

This one’s for the Fleetwood Mac fans. Lord Huron’s particular brand of indie folk draws clear inspiration from the classic folk rock band, with occasional twists of outlaw country and modern touches that are incredibly refreshing and very pleasing to the ear.

Fleetwood Mac - Rumours (1977)

Well deserving of its #7 spot on the Rolling Stone list, there is not one of Rumours’ 11 tracks that isn’t amazing. The stylistic range of the record from “Dreams” to “Don’t Stop” to “The Chain” is exemplary, all the while it still manages to feel uniform and complete.

Billy Joel - An Innocent Man (1983)

Billy Joel appears to be incapable of releasing a bad album. While An Innocent Man does not appear on the Rolling Stone list, it does feature even more of his best known songs, such as “The Longest Time” and “Uptown Girl”.

The Beatles - Abbey Road (1969)

Abbey Road is, according to Rolling Stone (and quite convincingly), the third greatest album of all time. It is The Beatles’ most iconic album, with a high density of some of their most widely known hits, including “Come Together,” “Something,” and “Oh! Darling,” to name a few. If, for some reason, you haven’t heard it, this is one for the bucket list for sure.

Father of Peace - The Year of Madness (2024)

Father of Peace stands out for their immediately recognizable style, both visually and musically. Their guitar arrangements are reminiscent of System of a Down, but with a sensibility and feelgoodery that lands them nearer the classic rock court. No one does it quite like them.

Billy Joel - The Stranger (1977)

At #169 on the Rolling Stone list, The Stranger is far and away Joel’s best album, featuring some of his most iconic tracks, including but not limited to “Movin’ Out (Anthony’s Song),” “Vienna,” “Only the Good Die Young,” and somehow still more.

The Who - Tommy (1969)

The Who’s most iconic album, it tells the story of an eponymous protagonist who, despite his traumatically induced deafness, blindness, and muteness, becomes a pinball superstar. At #190 on the Rolling Stone list, this bizarre but captivating rock opera has earned all of its acclaim and adaptations for the screen and stage.

The Beatles - Revolver (1966)

Just shy of the top 10, at #11 on the Rolling Stone list, Revolver represents, in my opinion, the second most Beatles-sounding Beatles album. Into a modest 34 minute runtime, they manage to stuff so many iconic tracks such as “Eleanor Rigby,” “I’m Only Sleeping,” “Yellow Submarine,” and myriad B-sides, and not a single skip.

The Beatles - Sgt. Pepper’s Lonely Hearts Club Band (1967)

Sgt. Pepper came near the end of The Beatles’ time together, when they weren’t really playing live anymore. As such, they had much more freedom to experiment with the songs without having to worry about how they would perform them. This freedom led to some of their strangest and most groundbreaking music, which lands this record at an impressive #24 on the Rolling Stone list.

Queen - A Night at the Opera (1975)

“Bohemian Rhapsody” is on here, as are “You’re My Best Friend” and perhaps the greatest love song of all time: “I’m In Love With My Car.” At #128 on the Rolling Stone list, with an impressive ratio of hits to B-side bangers, this record is some of Queen’s best and most iconic work.

Pink Floyd - The Wall (1979)

It’s Pink Floyd. It’s implicitly awesome. With an impressive 80 minute runtime across two discs, it represents some of Pink Floyd’s best and most iconic work, including the “Another Brick in the Wall” trilogy, “Hey You,” “Comfortably Numb,” and everything in between. At #129 on the Rolling Stone list, The Wall is one of those albums you have to listen to at least once in your life.

The Classic Collector

You think Zeppelin, Queen, and The Beatles were the greatest bands of all time (and I’m not here to tell you that’s wrong.) You love albums like Abbey Road, The Wall, and The Doors. Albums and artists you enjoy have an unmistakable twang and groove characteristic of analog recording and production techniques. Gotta love that grain!
